About HPOGraph — how this works
Click any question below to expand it.
Why show a graph instead of a tree?

Most HPO browsers, including HPO's own official site, render the ontology as a tree: pick one parent, draw a branch, repeat. That hides real information, because HPO is actually a directed acyclic graph (DAG) — a term can be reached through more than one parent branch. For example, "Abnormality of the hand" is reachable both via limb → upper limb → hand and via skeletal system → limb bone → ... — two genuinely different, both-valid ways of categorizing the same finding.

For a clinician choosing the best-fitting HPO term for a finding, seeing only one branch can be misleading — it looks like there's a single "correct" place in the hierarchy when there are several legitimate ones. The graph view here always shows the full ancestor closure of the term you've selected (every path back to the root, not just one) plus its direct children, so the multi-parent structure is visible rather than hidden.

How do search and the graph view work?

Search matches against a term's own name and its synonyms, with matches in the term's own name always ranked above synonym-only matches, then sorted alphabetically.

Selecting a result draws that term's neighborhood: every ancestor on every path back to the root (teal nodes), the term itself (dark node), and its direct children (outlined nodes). Click any node to make it the new focus and reveal its own neighborhood; double-click a node — or use the "+ Add to selected" button — to add it to your phenotype set on the right.

How are diseases and genes ranked?

For your selected phenotype set, each candidate disease is scored by how similar its own annotated phenotypes are to yours. Two phenotypes count as "similar" if they share a specific (rather than generic) common ancestor in the ontology — e.g. "Arachnodactyly" and "Long fingers" score highly similar, while "Arachnodactyly" and "Seizure" score near zero because their only shared ancestor is the near-meaningless root, "Phenotypic abnormality".

Concretely: for every term you selected, we find its single best-matching term on the candidate disease (and vice versa, for balance), then average those best-match scores — weighted by how rare/specific each matching term is, and by how often the disease's phenotype is annotated as frequent vs. rare. This is the same family of method used by phenotype-matching tools like Phenomizer and LIRICAL. Gene scores come from each gene's best-supporting linked disease. Click any disease or gene row to see exactly which terms matched and why.

Diseases are split into separate OMIM / Orphanet sub-tabs since the two databases curate disease definitions with different conventions and aren't always directly comparable.

What is the "informativeness rank" shown next to a term?

Separately from ranking diseases, every HPO term has its own standalone specificity score — roughly, "how gene/disease-specific is this exact term on its own", combining how many genes are linked to it, how many diseases annotate it, and how consistently it's reported as a frequent (vs. rare/occasional) finding. This is shown as "informativeness rank #N of ~20,400" in the focus bar. It's a reference number about the term itself, separate from — and not used by — the disease/gene ranking above.

How does the phenotype-set distance calculator work?

Selecting two or more terms reveals a "Phenotype set relationships" panel: how many distinct organ systems your selection spans, and a pairwise closeness/distance between every pair of selected terms, along with their most informative shared ancestor.

A distance near 0 means two terms are essentially the same finding described two ways (or one is a more specific version of the other). A distance near 1 means their only common ground is a very generic ancestor — they're phenotypically unrelated on their own. A selection that spans many organ systems, with high pairwise distances throughout, is itself a signal worth noticing: it points toward a syndromic, multi-system condition rather than an isolated finding, independent of what the disease/gene ranking says.

What does the "Suggest" tab do?

The Suggest tab proposes additional HPO terms you haven't selected yet, based on the phenotype overlap of your current top candidate diseases — it never uses gene/variant databases, and nothing leaves the browser. "Common to leaders" surfaces findings shared by almost all of your leading candidates, useful for confirming a working hypothesis. "Narrow it down" surfaces findings present in roughly half your leading candidates, which would meaningfully split the field if present or absent — useful for actively differentiating between top candidates. Each suggestion has a one-click "+ Add" button. These are suggestions to consider, not findings to accept uncritically — always confirm against the patient/record before adding a term you didn't directly observe.

What is ClinGen, and how is it used here?

ClinGen (Clinical Genome Resource) publishes independent, expert-panel-curated gene-disease validity classifications (Definitive/Strong/Moderate/Limited/Disputed/Refuted) — a genuinely different kind of evidence from the phenotype-similarity ranking above, reflecting overall confidence in a gene-disease relationship rather than a score for your specific patient.

Where possible, ClinGen data is matched to one specific candidate disease using the Mondo Disease Ontology's official exact-match crosswalk to OMIM/Orphanet — this covers the large majority of candidate diseases. Where no exact match exists, HPOGraph falls back to a gene-level signal: the best classification among any gene linked to that disease (less precise, since one gene can be linked to several diseases). You'll see this as a badge directly on disease/gene rows, a dedicated ClinGen tab with full curation detail, and — for stronger classifications only — a tag on the "Top matches so far" summary cards. ClinGen data is optional and only covers a subset of genes/diseases; its absence for a given row is not itself negative evidence.

Where does the data come from, and what are the limitations?

The ontology graph, disease annotations (with frequency/onset/evidence), and gene-disease associations come from the HPO Consortium's official releases (hpo.jax.org), cross-referenced with HGNC gene metadata. Gene-disease validity curations (optional) come from ClinGen, matched to specific diseases using the Mondo Disease Ontology's exact-match crosswalk (optional). Everything is compiled offline into one SQLite file that ships with the app and runs entirely in your browser — no data you search or select ever leaves your device.

Known limitations: candidate disease matching pre-filters using ancestor and descendant overlap, so a disease whose closest relation to a selected term is a "cousin" term reached only sideways through a different branch could occasionally be missed — this is rare in practice for clinically specific term selections. There's no per-term severity/onset input yet. ClinGen data doesn't cover every gene or disease, and gene-level fallback matching (see above) is less precise than a direct disease match. Your selected phenotype set lives in this browser tab, but can be shared via the page's URL or exported as JSON/PDF (see the Selected tab).
